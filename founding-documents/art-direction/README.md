# Founding Art Direction Guide

## Working Vision

This game should feel like **a living pencil drawing of a quiet golf course**.

The world is minimal, restrained, and nearly monochrome. It is not trying to look photorealistic. Instead, it should capture the *feeling* of a beautiful natural place through delicate lines, subtle texture, graceful motion, and believable physical response.

At rest, the course should resemble a carefully composed illustration on paper. In motion, it should feel as though that illustration has gently come alive.

The central promise of the visual style is:

> **A calm hand-drawn landscape where every movement leaves a subtle, natural trace.**

---

# 1. Core Emotional Tone

The game should feel:

- quiet
- contemplative
- natural
- tactile
- elegant
- spacious
- slightly nostalgic
- precise without feeling sterile
- playful without becoming cartoonish
- beautiful without becoming ornate

The experience should evoke the feeling of opening an old field guide, architectural sketchbook, or hand-illustrated course map and discovering that the drawing can move.

The player should feel that the world is calm, but not static. Grass bends. Water reacts. Sand shifts. The ball leaves a line through the environment. Everything responds gently.

---

# 2. Primary Art Pillars

## 2.1 Minimal Information, Maximum Character

Every mark should have a purpose.

The environment should avoid dense detail, visual noise, or decorative clutter. Character comes from:

- silhouette
- line rhythm
- texture density
- negative space
- subtle terrain contours
- physical reactions
- small imperfections

A single curved contour line can communicate a hill. A few clustered strokes can suggest tall grass. Several soft horizontal lines can establish water.

Do not fully render every surface. Let the viewer complete the image.

## 2.2 Drawn, Not Digitally Perfect

The art should feel made by a human hand, even when generated procedurally.

Lines should be controlled but never mechanically perfect. Curves may have tiny variations. Repeated marks should differ slightly in spacing, angle, or length. Shapes should feel observed and drawn rather than mathematically outlined.

The style is not messy sketching. It is **clean draftsmanship with natural imperfection**.

## 2.3 Physics as Visual Beauty

Physics is not only a gameplay system. It is part of the art direction.

When an object interacts with the world, the environment should answer:

- a rolling ball presses a faint path through tall grass
- a landing ball makes a small indentation
- sand produces a soft burst and a short trail
- water produces expanding rings
- a golf cart leaves paired tracks
- grass bends and slowly settles
- a ball striking a slope changes direction naturally
- a ball entering a hole produces a subtle final motion

These reactions should be delicate, readable, and satisfying. They should never become explosive, exaggerated, or visually loud.

## 2.4 Natural Restraint

The course should feel beautiful because of composition and motion, not because of effects.

Avoid:

- dramatic bloom
- glossy materials
- high saturation
- large particle effects
- cinematic lens effects
- heavy fog
- hard shadows
- dense post-processing
- excessive animation

The world should remain calm even during impact.

---

# 3. Color System

## 3.1 Default Palette

The game should use two or three primary tones:

1. **Paper / Ground Tone**  
   Warm off-white, pale stone, soft parchment, or quiet gray.

2. **Drawing Tone**  
   Charcoal gray, graphite, dark brown-gray, or softened black.

3. **Optional Midtone**  
   A restrained gray used for fairways, water, deep rough, or elevation separation.

The palette should remain nearly monochrome.

## 3.2 Palette Character

Colors should feel:

- matte
- dry
- natural
- slightly warm
- low contrast
- low saturation

Pure white and pure black should be used sparingly. Most of the image should live in gentle values between them.

## 3.3 Environmental Variants

Different courses may shift the base palette while preserving the same tonal relationships.

Examples:

- warm parchment and charcoal
- cool gray paper and graphite
- pale sand and dark umber
- fog white and soft blue-gray
- muted sage-gray and near-black

A biome may alter the paper tone, but it should not introduce a full-color illustrative style.

---

# 4. Line Language

## 4.1 Primary Outline

Major forms use thin, confident contour lines:

- fairway boundaries
- green edges
- bunker edges
- shorelines
- cliff faces
- cart silhouettes
- rocks
- plants
- tee boxes

Outlines should be visible but not heavy. No thick cartoon borders.

## 4.2 Hatching

Hatching communicates:

- slope
- depth
- surface direction
- shadow
- roughness
- elevation change

Hatching should follow the shape of the terrain. It should wrap around hills and descend into valleys rather than appearing as a flat screen texture.

Use hatching lightly. The drawing should still breathe.

## 4.3 Stippling

Small dots and broken marks can indicate:

- dry soil
- sandy rough
- fine grass texture
- distance
- sparse vegetation
- transitional areas between surfaces

Stippling should become less dense toward empty space.

## 4.4 Broken Lines

Not every edge should be continuous.

Terrain boundaries may disappear briefly into texture. Grass edges can dissolve. Waterlines may be interrupted by stones or reeds.

Broken edges make the world feel natural and prevent the course from looking like a vector diagram.

---

# 5. Terrain and Elevation

Terrain height should be readable without complex lighting or realistic 3D rendering.

## 5.1 Gentle Hills

Mild hills are shown through:

- curved contour hatching
- subtle compression of texture
- slight changes in surface value
- soft overlap of terrain lines
- the visible path of objects moving over the surface

Hills should feel broad and playable rather than mountainous.

## 5.2 Directional Slopes

A slope should have a clear directional flow.

Use:

- lines that bend downhill
- texture that follows the grade
- sparse shadow hatching on the lower side
- ball behavior that confirms the visual information

The player should be able to infer the likely roll direction by looking at the terrain.

## 5.3 Ridges

Ridges are communicated through a long raised curve with hatching falling away on both sides.

Keep ridges smooth and minimal. Avoid sharp, noisy triangulation.

## 5.4 Valleys and Swales

A swale should feel like a shallow bowl carved into the fairway.

Use inward-curving contour lines and slightly denser texture near the lowest point. Ball motion through a swale should feel smooth and inevitable.

## 5.5 Green Contours

Greens should have subtle internal shaping:

- gentle crowns
- shallow bowls
- side slopes
- tier transitions
- raised shoulders
- soft run-off areas

The green should not rely on arrows or overt overlays during normal play. Its drawn contour language and the movement of the ball should reveal its form.

Optional slope visualization may appear only as a temporary player aid.

---

# 6. Surface Types

## 6.1 Putting Green

The green is the calmest and cleanest surface.

Characteristics:

- shortest visual texture
- widest areas of negative space
- sparse stippling
- smooth outer contour
- extremely subtle internal hatching
- soft, readable elevation changes

The green should feel carefully maintained without appearing glossy or synthetic.

## 6.2 Fairway

The fairway is lightly textured and visually softer than the rough.

Characteristics:

- fine directional grain
- faint repeated strokes
- soft value fill
- broad, clean shape
- gentle mowing direction without literal striping

The fairway should guide the eye through the composition.

## 6.3 First Cut

The first cut is a narrow transition between fairway and rough.

It can be shown with slightly denser and longer strokes than the fairway, but should not need a dark outline on both sides.

## 6.4 Rough

Rough uses irregular clusters of short grass marks.

Characteristics:

- uneven density
- varied direction
- broken edges
- more visual resistance
- mild ball trail when crossed

It should feel natural and playable, not chaotic.

## 6.5 Deep Rough

Deep rough is taller, denser, and more animated.

Use:

- longer tapered strokes
- small overlapping grass clusters
- visible bending around the ball
- slower settling after contact
- localized movement rather than constant waving

The ball may become partially obscured, but never unreadable.

## 6.6 Dry Ground

Dry ground uses stippling, tiny stones, and sparse plant clusters.

It should feel open and quiet. Avoid filling every empty area.

## 6.7 Sand

Sand should be represented with:

- clean bunker contours
- fine parallel or curved rake lines
- soft edge hatching
- sparse granular marks
- visible impact traces

After a shot, sand may show:

- a small landing crater
- a brief spray of line particles
- a drag or roll trace
- a club mark
- footprints where appropriate

Marks can fade slowly or remain for the duration of the hole.

---

# 7. Water Features

Water should be one of the most visually restrained elements.

## 7.1 Still Water

Still water uses:

- fine horizontal lines
- large open areas
- occasional line interruptions
- a simple shoreline
- minimal reflected texture

Do not use glossy reflections or complex transparency.

## 7.2 Lakes and Ponds

Lakes should feel like calm negative space within the course.

Their shape should carry most of their identity. Use only enough internal linework to distinguish water from ground.

Ponds may have softer, rounder edges. Larger lakes can use longer, quieter horizontal strokes.

## 7.3 Streams

Streams use narrow, flowing contours and sparse directional lines.

The line rhythm should follow the current. Small rocks may interrupt the water, producing tiny curved ripples.

## 7.4 Coastal Water

Coastal water should remain minimal even when the shoreline is dramatic.

Use:

- long horizontal line fields
- occasional broken wave lines
- a strong but delicate cliff edge
- sparse rocks in the water
- no detailed ocean rendering

The sea should feel expansive because of emptiness, not detail.

## 7.5 Water Interaction

When the ball enters water:

1. the ball crosses the surface
2. a small, clean splash mark appears
3. two or three soft ripple rings expand
4. the rings lose opacity and line continuity
5. the surface returns to calm

The effect should feel poetic rather than comedic.

---

# 8. Cliffs, Banks, and Edges

## 8.1 Cliff Faces

Cliffs use vertical and diagonal hatching to indicate exposed material.

Characteristics:

- clear top silhouette
- broken, imperfect edge
- vertical fracture lines
- sparse shadow beneath overhangs
- minimal rock debris

Cliffs should feel grounded and natural without becoming highly detailed geological illustrations.

## 8.2 Raised Banks

Raised banks are softer than cliffs.

Use a curved upper contour and short downward hatching. Banks can frame greens, bunkers, or fairways.

## 8.3 Shoreline Banks

Water edges may be:

- soft and grassy
- rocky
- sandy
- sharply cut
- cliff-like

Each should remain readable through a small change in line language rather than a large change in color.

---

# 9. Course Features

## 9.1 Tee Boxes

Tee boxes should be simple geometric or softly rounded pads.

Use:

- a clean outline
- minimal texture
- a small tee marker or ball
- slight elevation indication when raised

They should feel integrated into the terrain rather than placed on top of it.

## 9.2 Flags and Holes

The flag is one of the strongest graphic anchors.

It should use:

- a thin pole
- a small solid or lightly filled flag
- a subtle cup mark
- restrained movement in wind

The flag should remain readable at gameplay scale without becoming oversized.

## 9.3 Bunkers

Bunkers should be elegant negative shapes with a clear lip.

Variations may include:

- shallow fairway bunker
- deep greenside bunker
- narrow finger bunker
- broad waste area
- small pot bunker

The edge, internal hatching, and ball response should communicate depth.

## 9.4 Rocks and Plants

Environmental details should be sparse and carefully placed.

Use a small visual vocabulary:

- pebble
- stone
- boulder
- low grass clump
- tall grass clump
- shrub
- reed
- cactus or biome-specific plant
- small tree silhouette where appropriate

Avoid placing details evenly. Cluster them naturally and preserve large quiet areas.

---

# 10. Golf Cart and Character Direction

## 10.1 Golf Cart

The cart should feel like a tiny hand-drawn model inside the landscape.

It should be:

- recognizable at small scale
- constructed from simple shapes
- lightly detailed
- consistent with the same thin-line language
- physically grounded on the terrain

The cart should not become a highly rendered focal object.

## 10.2 Character

The golfer should be simplified but human.

Use:

- a clear silhouette
- a few clothing folds
- minimal facial detail
- natural posture
- small, readable gestures

The character should feel like part of the illustration, not a separate animated cartoon.

## 10.3 Cart Orientation

The cart needs readable versions for:

- forward
- backward
- left-facing
- right-facing
- three-quarter left
- three-quarter right
- uphill
- downhill
- turning
- parked

Wheel placement and body perspective should remain consistent.

## 10.4 Cart Tracks

Tracks appear as two faint parallel paths.

Their look changes by surface:

- fairway: very subtle compression
- rough: bent grass and broken strokes
- dry ground: dotted tire impressions
- sand: clear tread marks
- wet ground: slightly darker compressed lines

Tracks should curve naturally with steering and respond to slope. They may gradually fade.

---

# 11. Motion and Animation

## 11.1 General Motion Rule

All animation should feel eased, weighted, and slightly understated.

Nothing should snap unless it is a deliberate interface action.

## 11.2 The Golf Ball

The ball should be the cleanest moving object in the scene.

Its motion should communicate:

- speed
- spin
- slope
- friction
- bounce
- surface resistance

The ball may use a tiny dark mark, soft shadow, or subtle trailing indication to remain visible.

Avoid bright glow, thick trails, or exaggerated squash and stretch.

## 11.3 Grass Motion

Grass moves locally rather than as a global wave.

It reacts to:

- wind
- the golf ball
- the cart
- footsteps
- nearby impacts

Motion should be small and delayed. Taller grass bends more and settles more slowly.

## 11.4 Wind

Wind is communicated through the response of existing elements:

- flag movement
- grass lean
- small drifting particles
- reeds
- occasional water line variation

Do not add a large visible wind effect unless used as a temporary gameplay aid.

## 11.5 Camera Movement

Camera motion should feel like moving across a physical illustration.

Use:

- slow pans
- gentle tracking
- restrained zoom
- subtle parallax
- smooth follow behavior

Avoid rapid rotation, strong perspective distortion, or constant camera movement.

---

# 12. Marks, Traces, and Environmental Memory

The world should remember interaction for a short time.

Possible traces include:

- ball trails through dew or grass
- compressed rough
- sand marks
- cart tracks
- footprints
- pitch marks
- water ripples
- small displaced pebbles
- disturbed plants

These marks make the course feel physical and inhabited.

They should remain subtle. The terrain must never become visually cluttered after repeated play. Older traces may fade, simplify, or disappear.

---

# 13. Composition and Camera

## 13.1 View

The preferred camera is a high, slightly angled view that sits between:

- top-down course map
- isometric illustration
- miniature physical landscape

This view allows the player to understand course layout while still perceiving elevation.

## 13.2 Framing

The hole should read as a composed illustration.

Use:

- a clear visual path from tee to green
- generous margins
- asymmetry
- quiet negative space
- restrained environmental clusters
- one or two major natural anchors

The course should never feel packed edge-to-edge.

## 13.3 Scale

Objects must remain consistently scaled.

The flag, cart, golfer, rocks, plants, and bunkers should all reinforce the sense that the course is a miniature but believable place.

---

# 14. Lighting and Depth

Lighting should be implied primarily through line density and value rather than realistic illumination.

Use:

- soft ambient shading
- mild contact shadows
- hatching on downward faces
- slightly darker undersides
- subtle separation between overlapping forms

Avoid:

- sharp directional shadows
- dramatic sunset lighting
- metallic highlights
- glossy reflections
- high dynamic range
- volumetric effects

The course should feel evenly illuminated, like a drawing observed in soft daylight.

---

# 15. Interface Relationship

The interface should feel like an extension of the same object.

Possible UI language:

- thin graphite lines
- small serif or restrained sans-serif typography
- paper-toned panels
- simple icons drawn with the same stroke weight
- minimal text
- quiet transitions
- no glossy cards or bright gradients

The game world should remain visually dominant.

During aiming or terrain reading, overlays should be temporary, delicate, and removable. They should clarify the drawing rather than cover it.

---

# 16. Procedural Art Rules

When course elements are generated dynamically, variation should remain within a controlled visual grammar.

## 16.1 Variation Should Affect

- contour shape
- line spacing
- hatch direction
- texture density
- plant clustering
- rock scale
- edge interruption
- slope rhythm
- waterline length

## 16.2 Variation Should Not Affect

- core stroke weight
- overall palette
- degree of visual restraint
- object scale
- readability
- physics language
- amount of detail beyond the established range

Procedural generation should create many drawings by the same imagined artist, not many unrelated styles.

---

# 17. Style Boundaries

## The Style Is

- a moving course sketch
- minimal editorial illustration
- pencil and ink terrain drawing
- quiet monochrome landscape
- tactile procedural art
- restrained natural animation
- readable physical terrain
- elegant environmental storytelling

## The Style Is Not

- photorealistic golf
- cel-shaded cartoon golf
- colorful arcade golf
- dense fantasy illustration
- technical CAD visualization
- noisy hand-scribbled sketching
- retro pixel art
- watercolor painting
- glossy mobile-game rendering
- a static sepia filter over ordinary 3D graphics

---

# 18. Decision Test

When evaluating any new visual element, ask:

1. Does it feel hand-drawn?
2. Does it preserve negative space?
3. Does it communicate through line, shape, and motion before color?
4. Is the physical response believable?
5. Is the effect quieter than it could be?
6. Does it belong to the same imagined sketchbook?
7. Does it make the course feel more alive without making it busier?
8. Would the element still look beautiful in a still frame?
9. Does it remain readable at gameplay scale?
10. Can any detail be removed without losing meaning?

When uncertain, simplify.

---

# 19. Founding Style Statement

> The game is a calm, living golf illustration rendered in a restrained pencil-and-ink language. Its terrain is formed through elegant contours, soft hatching, sparse texture, and generous negative space. The world responds physically to every action: grass bends, sand shifts, water ripples, tracks remain, and the ball rolls naturally across visible slopes. Every movement should feel quiet, tactile, and intentional. The goal is not realism through detail, but realism through behavior—and beauty through restraint.
