# Founding Player Experience Guide

## Working Experience Vision

This game should feel like **being allowed to wander inside a living golf illustration**.

The player is not moved from hole to hole, told what to do, or measured by an interface. They are placed in a quiet, open golf landscape and trusted to explore it. They may walk, drive, practice, play a full round, skip holes, hit several balls, follow wildlife, or simply stand somewhere beautiful and watch the course move around them.

The game should feel closer to the analog experience of spending an afternoon on a golf course than to operating a conventional sports game.

The central player promise is:

> **You are free to inhabit the course, and the course quietly responds to everything you do.**

---

# 1. The Experience in One Paragraph

The player enters an open golf landscape with no score display, no mission text, no minimap, no hole counter, and no instructions covering the world. They move their golfer naturally through the course, approach any ball, choose where to stand, line up a shot through body position and motion, and strike the ball into a fully continuous environment. The ball is never forced back into a prescribed play area. It may land on another fairway, roll down a hill, disappear into deep grass, strike a rock, enter water, or come to rest in an unimportant corner of the property. The player may continue from there, abandon it, or place another ball. Golf is treated not as a sequence of scored challenges, but as a physical activity inside a beautiful place.

---

# 2. Founding Experience Principles

## 2.1 Freedom Before Progression

The player should never feel that they must complete the course in the intended order.

They may:

- begin at any hole
- leave a hole halfway through
- play backward
- hit from one hole toward another
- practice around a green
- drive the cart without playing
- walk the entire course
- place additional balls
- replay the same shot
- explore environmental areas
- keep their own score outside the game
- ignore score entirely

The course should support self-directed play rather than enforce a formal round.

## 2.2 The World Is the Interface

The course itself should communicate what can be done.

A ball invites striking.  
A cart invites riding.  
A path invites following.  
A flag suggests a destination.  
A tee box suggests a beginning.  
A bunker communicates resistance through its shape and texture.  
Water communicates danger through its presence and motion.

The game should avoid replacing these physical cues with labels, prompts, arrows, or icons.

## 2.3 No Text During Play

During normal play, the screen should contain:

- no score
- no hole number
- no objective
- no button prompt
- no club name
- no power meter
- no minimap
- no waypoint
- no wind number
- no distance number
- no floating interaction label
- no tutorial paragraph
- no success banner

The ideal play screen is only the world.

Any essential system information should be conveyed through:

- posture
- animation
- sound
- object behavior
- camera framing
- physical positioning
- subtle changes in line weight or motion

## 2.4 Analog Understanding

The player should learn the game by observing cause and effect.

They should gradually understand:

- how far a normal swing sends the ball
- how slope changes roll
- how rough reduces speed
- how sand changes impact
- how the golfer aligns with the target
- how cart momentum behaves
- how the environment reacts to movement
- where a shot is likely to travel

The game should reward familiarity rather than numerical optimization.

## 2.5 Realism Through Consequence

The world should not simulate everything, but the things it does simulate should matter.

A shot should feel believable because:

- the golfer must physically reach the ball
- body position affects direction
- terrain affects stance
- club impact has weight
- slope affects bounce and roll
- surfaces have distinct friction
- water and sand visibly respond
- the ball remains where it actually lands

The game should avoid invisible corrections that make every shot conform to a conventional hole structure.

## 2.6 Calm, Not Empty

The absence of interface should not make the game feel unfinished.

The world remains alive through:

- birds landing and leaving
- grass shifting
- flags moving
- water rippling
- cart tracks
- drifting debris
- distant wildlife
- small weather changes
- the player’s physical traces
- the sound of footsteps, wind, and impact

The quiet landscape should continually provide small reasons to look and listen.

---

# 3. The Player Fantasy

The player is not primarily a competitor.

They are:

- a golfer
- a wanderer
- a caretaker of their own experience
- a visitor in a living landscape
- an observer of physical detail
- the author of their own round

The fantasy is not “win the tournament.”

The fantasy is:

> “Spend time somewhere beautiful, hit golf balls, and see what happens.”

---

# 4. Structure of Play

## 4.1 Arrival

The player should enter the world gently.

Possible opening:

- the course fades in from paper texture
- ambient sound arrives before movement
- the golfer is already standing near a simple starting area
- a ball is visible nearby
- a golf cart rests within walking distance
- the first flag can be seen, but nothing demands that the player approach it

The game should not begin with a menu-heavy sequence or tutorial overlay.

## 4.2 Wandering

Walking is a first-class activity, not downtime between shots.

The player should enjoy:

- moving across hills
- following cart paths
- walking around ponds
- approaching cliffs
- crossing between holes
- inspecting the course from different angles
- discovering small environmental events
- watching the world respond beneath their feet

Walking should be slow enough to feel grounded, but not tedious.

## 4.3 Encountering a Ball

A ball should be understandable without an interaction prompt.

As the player approaches:

- the golfer’s attention subtly shifts toward it
- their walking speed eases
- their posture becomes more deliberate
- the camera may settle slightly
- the golfer naturally positions near the ball

The player should feel that the character recognizes the ball as an actionable object.

## 4.4 Preparing a Shot

Preparing a shot should feel embodied rather than menu-driven.

The player should:

- approach the ball
- move around it
- establish a facing direction
- settle into a stance
- make a physical swing input
- watch the result

The course should remain fully visible throughout the process.

## 4.5 Following the Ball

After impact, the camera should help the player appreciate the shot without removing their sense of place.

Possible behavior:

1. briefly hold the golfer and initial launch in frame
2. track the ball smoothly
3. preserve enough terrain context to read height and direction
4. slow as the ball loses speed
5. settle near the final resting location
6. allow the player to resume control naturally

The transition should not feel like entering a separate “shot mode.”

## 4.6 Continuing or Abandoning

When the ball stops, the player chooses what happens next.

They may:

- walk to it
- drive to it
- hit another ball
- leave it behind
- return to a previous area
- place a new ball
- continue toward a flag
- begin an entirely different hole

The game should not judge abandonment.

# 5. Open-World Golf Rules

## 5.1 One Continuous Course

The entire playable property should exist as one continuous environment.

Avoid:

- loading screens between holes
- invisible walls around fairways
- automatic relocation after a shot
- out-of-bounds resets
- forced tee order
- separate isolated hole scenes

A ball can travel from one hole into another and remain there.

## 5.2 No Sacred Playing Corridor

The fairway is an invitation, not a constraint.

The player should be able to hit into:

- adjacent fairways
- cart paths
- rough
- deep grass
- maintenance areas
- rocky ground
- shoreline
- another green
- woodland edges
- practice areas
- unusual terrain

Only genuine physical barriers should stop movement.

## 5.3 Balls Are Physical Objects

Every active ball should have:

- a real position
- a surface relationship
- a visible resting state
- believable collision and friction
- persistence until removed or reset

The game may limit the total number of active balls for performance, but this limit should be handled quietly.

## 5.4 Spawning Additional Balls

The player should be able to place another ball without opening a conventional inventory.

Possible no-text approaches:

- the golfer reaches into a pocket and places a ball
- holding a simple input causes a ball-placement gesture
- standing still and making a repeated gesture places a practice ball
- a golf bag physically contains additional balls
- a caddy or cart supplies balls through animation

The action should feel like something the golfer does, not a debug command.

## 5.5 No Automatic Score

The game should not track or display score during the first version.

There is no need for:

- par
- strokes
- penalties
- scorecard
- leaderboard
- hole completion state
- round completion state
- achievements
- reward currency

Players who care about score may keep it themselves.

This reinforces the analog nature of the experience and removes pressure from experimentation.

# 6. Movement Experience

## 6.1 Cursor-Led Walking

The preferred movement concept is direct, spatial, and low-interface:

- the cursor indicates a destination or movement direction
- pressing and holding causes the golfer to walk toward it
- releasing causes the golfer to slow and stop
- the golfer turns naturally before moving
- speed builds gently rather than instantly
- the character follows terrain elevation
- nearby obstacles influence the path

This should feel like guiding a person through a miniature landscape.

## 6.2 Movement Interpretation

Cursor distance may influence behavior:

- cursor close to golfer: turn, adjust, or walk slowly
- cursor moderately far: normal walking
- cursor far from golfer: brisk walk
- optional secondary input: jog

The screen should not show a destination marker.

## 6.3 Terrain Navigation

The golfer should:

- walk up and down hills
- slow slightly on steep slopes
- step naturally across uneven ground
- avoid entering deep water
- route around cliff edges
- cross shallow terrain transitions
- enter bunkers deliberately
- move through rough with mild resistance

Pathfinding should preserve player intent. It should not produce long, surprising detours.

## 6.4 Bunkers

The player may walk into bunkers.

Movement should communicate the surface:

- shorter steps
- slight foot sinking
- subtle footprints
- slower acceleration
- soft sand sound

The golfer should not automatically path around a bunker if the player clearly directs them into it.

## 6.5 Water

Deep water should be treated as a physical boundary.

The golfer may stop near the edge and adjust naturally. The game should not need an invisible warning icon.

Shallow shoreline areas may permit stepping or wading only if this fits the intended tone and animation scope.

## 6.6 Cliffs and Dangerous Edges

The player should not accidentally walk off a cliff because of imprecise input.

Near dangerous edges:

- movement slows slightly
- pathfinding respects the boundary
- the golfer adjusts footing
- direct impossible movement is translated into movement along the edge

This safety should feel like natural human caution rather than a collision wall.

---

# 7. Camera Experience

## 7.1 The Camera as an Observer

The camera should feel like a calm observer floating above a hand-drawn miniature.

It should not feel like:

- a broadcast camera
- a drone racing through the course
- a rigid top-down strategy camera
- a dramatic cinematic camera
- a first-person sports camera

## 7.2 Walking Camera

While walking:

- center loosely around the golfer
- look slightly ahead in the direction of movement
- ease over terrain height changes
- preserve a stable horizon and visual orientation
- reveal nearby landmarks naturally
- avoid constant rotation

## 7.3 Shot Camera

During a shot:

- preserve the golfer’s swing
- transition smoothly toward the ball
- keep the ball readable
- show terrain influence
- avoid exaggerated zoom
- settle without snapping

## 7.4 Player Camera Freedom

The player should be able to inspect the landscape.

Useful actions may include:

- gentle pan
- restrained zoom
- slow rotation
- recenter on golfer
- recenter on current ball

These actions should work without visible camera controls.

## 7.5 Camera Restraint

Avoid:

- camera shake on normal impacts
- rapid cuts
- slow-motion by default
- extreme close-ups
- automatic beauty shots that interrupt control
- aggressive cinematic framing

The shot itself should provide the spectacle.

---

# 8. The Shot Experience

## 8.1 Core Goal

A golf shot should feel like one continuous physical action:

> approach, align, settle, swing, impact, flight, bounce, roll, rest.

The player should not feel that they entered a separate minigame.

## 8.2 Aiming Without an Aim Line

The game should avoid a projected trajectory line.

Direction can be communicated through:

- the golfer’s feet
- shoulder alignment
- club face orientation
- head and eye direction
- camera composition
- a practice swing
- the position of the cursor relative to the ball

The player learns to read the golfer’s body as the aiming interface.

## 8.3 Entering a Stance

When near a ball, the player can begin shot preparation through a simple input.

The golfer then:

- approaches the correct side
- places their feet
- grips the club
- settles their weight
- looks toward the intended direction

The animation should remain interruptible. The player can step out of stance and reposition.

## 8.4 Direction Adjustment

While in stance, cursor movement can rotate the golfer around the ball.

Possible model:

- horizontal cursor movement adjusts shot direction
- the golfer steps subtly around the ball
- feet and shoulders realign
- the club head remains grounded near the ball
- the camera gently favors the target direction

No compass, degrees, or arrow is shown.

## 8.5 Swing Input

The swing should be physical, legible, and learnable.

A strong candidate is a drag-and-release gesture:

1. press near the golfer or ball
2. pull backward to begin the backswing
3. release or move forward to swing
4. timing, length, and direction influence impact

The gesture should map to the rhythm of a real swing rather than a power bar.

Alternative candidate:

- press and hold to draw the club back
- release to swing
- cursor direction determines alignment

The system should prioritize grace and consistency over difficult timing.

## 8.6 Power

Power should be inferred from motion.

Communicate power through:

- backswing length
- golfer coil
- swing speed
- sound
- follow-through
- initial ball velocity

Do not show a percentage.

## 8.7 Accuracy

Accuracy may come from:

- alignment before the swing
- smoothness of the input
- timing through the impact point
- lie and terrain
- club behavior

The game should not punish the player with highly random dispersion.

Imperfect shots should feel physically understandable.

## 8.8 Practice Swing

A practice swing could be available through the same input performed away from the ball or with a modifier.

This would help players understand:

- rhythm
- direction
- animation timing
- power

The practice swing should disturb grass slightly but not strike the ball.

## 8.9 Impact

Impact should be one of the most satisfying moments in the game.

It should include:

- crisp but natural sound
- visible club contact
- slight grass or sand reaction
- immediate ball response
- grounded follow-through
- no exaggerated flash
- no floating “perfect” message

The player should know whether the strike was clean by watching and hearing it.

---

# 9. Ball Physics Experience

## 9.1 Physical Continuity

The ball should remain physically continuous from impact to rest.

Avoid hidden state changes between:

- launch
- flight
- bounce
- roll
- collision
- water entry

## 9.2 Surface Response

Each surface should be understandable through ball behavior.

### Green

- low resistance
- long, smooth roll
- highly responsive to slope
- very small bounce
- clear final settling

### Fairway

- moderate bounce
- predictable roll
- subtle grain influence if used
- clean visual trail only in special conditions

### Rough

- reduced roll
- softened bounce
- mild lateral disturbance
- bent grass around the resting ball

### Deep Rough

- abrupt speed loss
- partial visual concealment
- unpredictable but bounded settling
- strong grass reaction

### Sand

- dampened impact
- visible indentation
- short roll
- granular movement
- possible buried lies

### Cart Path or Hard Ground

- sharper bounce
- longer skip
- audible hard contact
- more energetic roll

### Water

- surface impact
- small splash
- expanding ripples
- ball submerges or disappears naturally

## 9.3 Terrain Influence

Hills should meaningfully affect the ball.

The player should be able to observe:

- downhill acceleration
- uphill deceleration
- side-slope break
- ridge deflection
- bowl collection
- swale guidance

The terrain drawing and ball behavior must agree.

## 9.4 Rest

The ball should settle gradually.

A ball at near-zero velocity may:

- make one final partial rotation
- shift slightly into grass
- nestle into sand
- stop against a stone
- drift a final inch down a slope

The end of motion should feel resolved, not abruptly clamped.

---

# 10. Golf Cart Experience

## 10.1 The Cart as Freedom

The cart is not merely fast travel.

It is part of the fantasy of inhabiting the course.

The player should be able to:

- enter it
- drive anywhere physically reachable
- follow cart paths
- cross fairways
- park near a ball
- leave it behind
- return to it later
- use it simply for exploration

## 10.2 Entering and Exiting

No interaction label should appear.

As the player approaches the driver side:

- the golfer turns toward the cart
- a subtle posture change indicates availability
- a single input begins the entry animation
- the camera adjusts naturally

Exiting should place the golfer on safe nearby ground.

## 10.3 Driving Feel

Driving should be:

- gentle
- responsive
- slightly weighty
- easy to understand
- slower on steep or rough terrain
- capable of smooth turns
- free from arcade drifting

The cart should feel pleasant enough that driving without a destination is enjoyable.

## 10.4 Cart and Terrain

The cart should:

- tilt mildly with slopes
- compress grass
- leave tracks
- bounce subtly on rough ground
- slow in sand
- avoid deep water
- respect cliff edges
- interact with small plants where appropriate

## 10.5 Parking

When stopped:

- the cart settles slightly
- wheels straighten or remain naturally angled
- tracks end beneath the tires
- the golfer exits without snapping
- the cart remains where parked

The world should remember its location.

---

# 11. Club Experience

## 11.1 First Iteration

For the earliest version, the player may use one general-purpose club.

This is acceptable if:

- the swing feels excellent
- the ball physics are expressive
- the course supports experimentation
- the experience remains coherent

The first iteration should not delay the core experience in order to build a complete equipment system.

## 11.2 Future Club Philosophy

When clubs are added, selection should remain physical and wordless.

Possible approaches:

### Physical Golf Bag

The player approaches a bag and selects a visible club by shape and length.

### Caddy

A caddy follows at a respectful distance and presents a small group of clubs when approached.

### Cart Bag

Clubs remain attached to the cart. The golfer physically replaces one and removes another.

### Gesture Wheel Without Text

A temporary radial arrangement shows club silhouettes only. The player chooses by shape.

## 11.3 Club Readability

Clubs should be distinguishable visually:

- driver: long shaft, large head
- iron: medium length, angled head
- wedge: shorter, open face
- putter: distinct compact head

The game should not require players to memorize tiny differences at gameplay scale. Shape language must be clear.

## 11.4 Recommended Direction

The strongest thematic direction is a **physical bag or caddy**, because it keeps equipment inside the world.

A non-text silhouette selector may be used as an accessibility fallback, but it should not become the primary fantasy.

---

# 12. Wildlife and Ambient Life

## 12.1 Wildlife as Quiet Events

Animals should make the course feel inhabited without becoming collectibles or objectives.

Examples:

- crows landing on a green
- birds walking near a bunker
- ducks moving across a pond
- insects rising briefly from grass
- a rabbit crossing a distant fairway
- fish creating small water rings
- birds lifting from trees as a cart passes
- a heron leaving the shoreline

## 12.2 Reactive Behavior

Wildlife should react to the player and ball.

Examples:

- crows fly away when a ball lands nearby
- birds step aside as the golfer approaches
- ducks move away from a splash
- insects scatter from tall grass
- a distant animal pauses at the sound of impact

These reactions should be subtle and unscripted in appearance.

## 12.3 No Wildlife UI

Wildlife should never trigger:

- labels
- discovery notifications
- collection progress
- achievements
- camera lock
- forced cutscenes

The reward is noticing it.

---

# 13. Environmental Response

## 13.1 The Player Leaves Traces

The world should show evidence of passage.

Possible traces:

- footprints in sand
- bent grass
- cart tracks
- pitch marks
- divots
- disturbed water
- displaced small stones
- compressed rough
- fallen grass strokes
- temporary dust

## 13.2 Persistence

Traces may persist based on importance:

- water ripples: seconds
- bent grass: several seconds
- dust: moments
- footprints: several minutes or until leaving the area
- cart tracks: long-lasting
- pitch marks: long-lasting
- divots: long-lasting during the session
- abandoned balls: persistent

Persistence makes the world feel analog and continuous.

## 13.3 Restraint

The course must not become cluttered.

Use:

- fading
- simplification
- distance-based removal
- limits on repeated marks
- merging of overlapping tracks
- lower contrast for older traces

The world remembers, but quietly.

---

# 14. Sound Experience

## 14.1 Sound Is Part of the Interface

Because the game has no text, sound must help explain state and action.

Important sounds include:

- footsteps by surface
- club moving through air
- clean impact
- poor impact
- ball on grass
- ball on sand
- ball on stone
- splash
- cart motor
- tire movement
- flag cloth
- nearby birds
- water movement
- wind through vegetation

## 14.2 Dynamic Quiet

The soundscape should preserve silence.

Avoid filling every moment with:

- constant music
- loud ambient loops
- frequent stingers
- reward sounds
- UI clicks
- verbal commentary

Quiet allows small physical sounds to become meaningful.

## 14.3 Music

Music, if present, should be sparse and optional.

Possible use:

- during arrival
- during weather changes
- at distant intervals
- at the end of a long period of play
- as a very low ambient layer

The course itself should provide the primary soundtrack.

---

# 15. No-Text Interaction Language

## 15.1 Readability Through Animation

An object is usable when the character acknowledges it.

Examples:

- near a ball: golfer looks down and adjusts stance
- near cart: golfer glances toward the seat
- near bag: golfer reaches toward clubs
- near water edge: golfer slows and adjusts footing
- ready to swing: hands settle and body becomes still

## 15.2 Readability Through Framing

The camera may subtly support an interaction by:

- centering the relevant object
- creating space in the intended direction
- reducing movement
- revealing the path ahead
- holding on an important reaction

This should remain nearly invisible.

## 15.3 Readability Through Sound

A quiet sound can confirm:

- stance entered
- club selected
- ball placed
- cart engaged
- shot struck
- ball holed

Confirmation should come from the object itself, not a generic interface sound.

---

# 16. Learning Without a Tutorial Overlay

## 16.1 Environmental Teaching

The starting area should teach through layout.

A good opening area may include:

- golfer
- nearby ball
- short visible fairway
- gentle hill
- obvious green and flag
- parked cart
- no severe hazards

The player can experiment safely.

## 16.2 Forgiving First Contact

The first swing should be easy to produce.

Early interactions should favor:

- readable direction
- moderate power
- low chance of catastrophic failure
- generous terrain
- clear reaction

The game may become more physically demanding through course design rather than obscure controls.

## 16.3 Repetition as Learning

Players learn by:

- making several swings
- observing distance
- comparing surfaces
- noticing slope
- repositioning around the ball
- trying different input rhythms

The game should make experimentation pleasant and inexpensive.

---

# 17. Failure and Recovery

## 17.1 There Is No Formal Failure

A bad shot is not a failure state.

It is simply a new situation.

The player may:

- continue from the lie
- place another ball
- walk away
- try again
- laugh at the result
- explore where it landed

## 17.2 Water Balls

When a ball enters water, the game should not immediately display a penalty or reset.

The ball may:

- sink
- remain faintly visible in shallow water
- drift slightly
- be considered abandoned

The player decides whether to place another ball.

## 17.3 Lost Balls

A ball in deep rough may be difficult to see.

The game may gently preserve usability through:

- a tiny glint
- grass displacement
- a subtle camera settle
- the golfer looking toward it
- a faint sound when nearby

Avoid a floating marker.

## 17.4 Inaccessible Balls

If a ball reaches physically inaccessible terrain, the player can leave it.

A ball-placement action is enough. No error screen is needed.

---

# 18. Pace and Rhythm

The intended rhythm is:

1. observe
2. move
3. settle
4. act
5. watch
6. listen
7. choose what to do next

The game should not hurry the player.

Avoid:

- countdowns
- turn timers
- forced transitions
- repeated reminders
- score pressure
- constant rewards
- overstimulating feedback

The player should be comfortable doing nothing for a moment.

---

# 19. Session Experience

## 19.1 A Session Has No Required Length

A satisfying session may be:

- five minutes of putting
- ten minutes driving the cart
- one complete hole
- an hour-long improvised round
- a walk around the coast
- repeated shots into a pond
- quietly watching a weather change

The game should not define a “correct” session.

## 19.2 Returning to the World

The game may remember:

- golfer position
- cart position
- active balls
- time of day
- temporary course marks
- recent weather
- chosen club

Returning should feel like resuming a place, not loading a level.

## 19.3 Gentle Reset

A reset should exist, but remain outside the normal play language.

It may be available through a pause screen or holding a deliberate input. It should never happen automatically because the player broke an assumed sequence.

---

# 20. Accessibility Without Visual Clutter

The no-text philosophy should not prevent accessibility.

Optional settings outside normal play may support:

- higher contrast
- larger ball rendering
- slower swing timing
- simplified swing input
- reduced camera motion
- alternate movement controls
- stronger sound cues
- reduced environmental animation
- visible aiming aid
- terrain slope overlay
- controller remapping

These aids should be user-controlled and absent by default.

The founding experience remains minimal, but the game should not confuse minimalism with exclusion.

---

# 21. Recommended First-Iteration Control Model

## 21.1 Movement

- move cursor toward desired location
- hold primary input to walk
- release to stop
- cursor distance influences walking pace
- camera follows softly
- pathfinding handles minor obstacles
- direct player intent overrides convenience routing

## 21.2 Ball Interaction

- approach ball
- press a dedicated shot input to enter stance
- move cursor to rotate alignment around ball
- pull backward to create backswing
- release or sweep forward to swing
- watch continuous ball physics
- regain walking control after the ball rests or through a cancel input

## 21.3 Ball Placement

- hold a secondary input while standing still
- golfer removes and places a new ball
- ball is placed on valid nearby ground
- no inventory count is shown

## 21.4 Cart

- approach driver side
- press interaction input
- steer directly with cursor or directional input
- press again to stop and exit
- cart remains where parked

## 21.5 Camera

- edge movement or drag to pan
- wheel or gesture to zoom
- optional input to recenter on golfer
- optional input to follow current ball

This model should be prototyped early because the entire experience depends on movement and shot preparation feeling natural without visible UI.

# 23. Experience Risks

## 23.1 Minimalism Can Become Confusion

Removing all UI is only successful if body language, camera, sound, and world design are exceptionally clear.

The solution is not to add text immediately. First improve the physical communication.

## 23.2 Freedom Can Feel Aimless

The world needs visual invitations:

- visible flags
- interesting terrain
- carts
- paths
- wildlife
- nearby balls
- distinct landmarks

The player should never be ordered, but they should often be gently tempted.

## 23.3 Realism Can Become Friction

Walking every long distance or repeatedly retrieving balls may become tedious.

The cart, ball placement, sensible camera behavior, and optional quality-of-life settings should preserve freedom without labor.

## 23.4 Physics Can Become Unreadable

Highly realistic ball behavior is not valuable if the player cannot understand why something happened.

Terrain shape, animation, sound, and surface texture must make causes visible.

## 23.5 Open World Can Dilute Art Direction

A larger world can become repetitive or visually noisy.

Each region should use:

- strong composition
- restrained landmarks
- intentional negative space
- distinct terrain rhythm
- controlled detail density

# 24. Experience Boundaries

## The Experience Is

- open-ended
- physical
- slow enough to observe
- responsive
- analog in spirit
- visually quiet
- playful
- exploratory
- self-directed
- grounded in place
- free from performance pressure

## The Experience Is Not

- a tournament simulator
- a mobile golf puzzle
- a sequence of isolated holes
- a score-chasing arcade game
- a guided narrative adventure
- a technical swing simulator
- a menu-driven sports game
- a sandbox full of unrelated activities
- a walking simulator with golf added afterward

# 25. Player Experience Decision Test

When evaluating a new feature, ask:

1. Does this increase the player’s freedom?
2. Does it make the course feel more physical?
3. Can the player understand it without text?
4. Does it belong naturally inside the world?
5. Does it preserve the calm visual field?
6. Does it reward observation?
7. Does it create a meaningful response to player action?
8. Does it avoid turning play into task completion?
9. Does it remain enjoyable without score?
10. Can the same goal be achieved with less interface?
11. Would a player discover it naturally?
12. Does it make simply spending time on the course more enjoyable?

When uncertain, prefer the option that feels more physical, more permissive, and less explicit.

# 26. Founding Player Experience Statement

> The player is placed inside a continuous, living golf landscape and given the freedom to inhabit it without instruction, scoring, or pressure. There are no prescribed holes, no out-of-bounds resets, and no interface competing with the world. The player walks, drives, places balls, takes shots, and follows the consequences wherever they lead. The game communicates through posture, motion, sound, terrain, and environmental response rather than text. Every action should leave a quiet trace, and every part of the course should invite curiosity. The experience is not primarily about finishing a round. It is about the simple pleasure of being somewhere beautiful and playing golf within it.
